As a start a description is given of what an Interactive Sound Sculpture is. The basic idea is that an Interactive Sound Sculpture is a device that produces sound variable in amplitude, frequency, and/or timbre, with an interactive interface. This is very similar to a musical instrument, they can be regarded as the same, however the purpose of the sound sculpture lies in the user input interaction part of it, whereas the purpose of an instrument is to create music. In addition to having an interactive input interface, the output interface should also be more interactive, sound alone can be difficult to distinguish. Using visual aids, the difference becomes much more apparent. This complete interface must be intuitive and also intriguing, so the experience becomes memorable and the concepts that are associated with the device are understood and absorbed by the user. To give some background, multiple source learning positively affects the ease of learning as long as the multichannel communication provides related or relevant information [@fleming1978instructional]. This means the addition of hearing sound that is equivalent to frequencies students learn about can positively impact their understanding of frequencies, waveform and even advanced topic like Fourier transforms.

The systemic description of a sound sculpture is given using the diagram in [](#fig-SimpleSystem). First, the user interaction is sensed, for example, a guitar string that is struck. The resulting signal is processed, the oscillation of the string at a certain frequency. And translated into an audible sound, this oscillation moves the air and gets amplified in the sound box of the guitar.

The intent of the device is that it will be used as an educative tool in learning concepts of sound, waves and frequency on a different level than given in a standard high school curriculum. The device will be part of an NLT [^NLT] @NLT_module module, where it provides an interactive experience with the concepts inside the module. The concepts explored include:

1. Sound: Time plots, different wave forms (sine, block, recorded samples).
1. Frequency: Deconstruction of simple wave forms and spectrograms of more complicated.
1. Music: Decay, attack, notes and layering.
1. Coding: Using simple python scripts to see and hear the concepts.
1. Simple circuitry: Basic oscillators.


## Problem Definition
In addition to an interactive text book for an NLT module, where concepts of sound, waves and frequency are explored, the client wants an interactive physical device. The target audience for the use of this device is high school students of 5/6 VWO. The device must, therefore, be intuitive, intriguing, educational and fit in the scope of the NLT module.

## Goal
The goal of this project is providing the client with a device which adds an intuitive, interactive way to connect the concepts of sound, waves and frequencies. To achieve this, possible interaction methods and existing educational solutions are investigated and evaluated. Based on the findings, a suitable concept is developed and prototyped into a physical device that supports the learning objectives of the NLT module.

The Theremin is often regarded as one of the earliest examples of a sound sculpture: an instrument in which sound is generated through spatial interaction rather than direct physical contact. Its principle of controlling pitch and volume through hand proximity to electromagnetic fields established an important foundation for later developed gesture controlled musical interfaces. Several other projects expand on this concept by replacing the original capacitive antennas with other sensing methods while preserving the idea of free space interaction.

One important example is the TheremUS. Instead of using radiofrequency heterodyning and capacitive antennas as in the classical Theremin, TheremUS uses ultrasonic distance sensing to detect hand position. This significantly improves flexibility in signal processing and allows the entire instrument to be implemented digitally. A major advantage is that different sound synthesis algorithms can be programmed directly into the system, and the instrument can interface with external media systems through MIDI communication. In addition, the project includes translucent RGB LED panels that allow visual mappings. With that they are extending the instrument into a multisensory sound sculpture rather than a purely musical interface [@TheremUS].

Another related project is the Termenova, which expands the Theremin concept into a broader human computer interaction framework. Rather than functioning solely as a musical instrument, Termenova combines gesture recognition with capacitive sensors with lidar techniques to create a hybrid interface for interaction that aims to improve performer control and precision compared to the original Theremin. [@Thermenova].

Another relevant work is the HaNdle, which investigates gesture driven sound sculpture in interactive installations. In this project, IR sensing is used to detect the distance and gesture of the hand. 
unlike the Theremin, the project does not put the focus on the accurate pitch and volume based on distance but more on gesture recognition and turning that into sound. This aligns closely with sound art installations, where the instrument becomes an experiential sculpture and the distinction between performer and observer becomes less defined [@tarabella_handle].

The project *TheremUS* can also be compared to more recent developments such as wearable Theremin-based sensing systems. Bello et al. (2022) demonstrated the use of conductive textile antennas embedded in clothing, allowing Theremin principles to be applied to posture and gesture recognition rather than music performance alone. They use capacitive sensing derived from the Theremin system to classify body movements with high accuracy, showing how Theremin-inspired sensing can move beyond musical applications into interactive wearable technology [@Textile].

From a technical perspective, these projects also address several limitations of the classical Theremin. Traditional Theremins are highly sensitive to environmental interference because their operation depends on small capacitance variations around the antenna. This makes stable pitch control difficult and limits precision. Ultrasonic systems such as TheremUS avoid some of these issues by using direct distance measurement rather than field disturbance, while hybrid digital systems improve calibration and reproducibility. As a result, many modern sound sculpture projects preserve the Theremin’s expressive spatial interaction while replacing its less stable analog sensing principle with more robust digital technologies [@PhysicsOfTheTheremin].

These examples demonstrate that the Theremin should not only be understood as a historical electronic instrument, but also as a conceptual predecessor of modern interactive sound sculpture. Its core idea sound controlled through movement in space continues to influence contemporary research in musical interfaces, interactive installations, and gesture based human-machine interaction. Modern projects such as TheremUS, Termenova, and HaNdle show how this principle can be reinterpreted using ultrasonic sensing, hybrid interfaces, and immersive sound art environments. However, what is lacking in these examples are parts that support the educational possibilities of a Theremin. 


Existing musical instruments do not provide a simple and intuitive way to connect
interactive user inputs with corresponding audio and visual feedback for educational purposes. This makes it difficult for VWO 5/6 students to understand how sound properties are influenced by physical interaction.

## Scoping and bounding
The sound sculpture will be used during lessons on high schools. It will help students to learn about complicated subjects surrounding (sound) waves. The sound sculpture will also help the schools and the teachers to make their lessons more fun and interactive for the students. The project therefore aims to deliver a working prototype, which can show the learning capabilities of the sound sculpture. 

The prototype will consist of a four main parts:
- A sensing system that detects user interaction
- A signal processing system that converts the measured input into audible sound
- A visualization system that will visualize the played sound. 
- A housing that integrates all the electronics into a save and contained space. 

The success of the project will be evaluated based on the prototype’s ability to clearly demonstrate the targeted learning concepts, its usability during classroom activities, and its ability to provide clear and responsive audio and visual feedback.

Several aspects are outside the scope of this project. The sound sculpture will not be used as a commercially viable product, or as a professional music instrument. A detailed manual or learning module will also not be provided during the project. The project is bounded by a development period of eight weeks. Therefore there is no time for extensive testing with high school students 


## System Subdivision
This project has been split into two subdivisions to eventually come together and create the end product. These division are as following:

* Hardware & Sensing: This subdivision is responsible on the sensing part of the hand, visualization of the wave and all the hardware of the system. This includes the housing, switches and speaker system of the product. 

* Signal Processing: This subdivision is responsible for interpreting the sensing readings and the sound generation according to these readings. The sound generation will include at least 3 different waveforms and autotuning will used to making playing the instrument easier. Calibration will also be part of the subdivision.
